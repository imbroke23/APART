Chapter 1: Art Before History

  • Concepts/ Main Ideas
    • Human expression existed across the globe before written records
    • Very early art was produced worldwide and shared certain features- particularly concerned with the natural world and humans‚Äô place within it- art provides insight into the beginnings of ritual life
    • 1st instances of important artistic media, approaches, and values that occurred on different continents- Africa and Asia precede and influence other areas
    • Prehistoric (Paleolithic) hunters attributed magical properties to the images they painted
    • In Neolithic times human beings began to settle into villages and domesticate animals and food grains- this changed Neolithic man‚Äôs view of himself and the world.
  • Paleolithic Period
    • Man‚Äôs earliest attempts to represent his ideas and feelings in works of art have their origins some 30, 000 years ago.
    • Variety of works produced- from simple shell necklaces to human and animal forms in ivory, clay, and stone to monumental paintings, engravings, and relief sculptures covering huge wall surfaces of caves
    • Move from recognition to representation
    • In caves in Southern France, Northern Spain, central Europe, Asia, and Africa see the earliest examples of man-made images and symbols.
    • Paleolithic cavemen felt that by imitating the shapes of animals and forms of the human body they had some power over these subjects.
  • Paleolithic Cave Paintings
    • Some of earliest come from Africa (Namibia)
    • Oldest paintings were portable objects (i.e. fig.1-2)
    • Made with mineral colors from local sources
    • Colors mixed with fat, animal blood, or some other medium
    • Crude instruments (i.e. reeds, bristle brushes, fur, moss pads) used to fill in scratched outlines of animal‚Äôs body
    • Wide range of color with the exception of blues and greens
    • Even with limited tools, Prehistoric artists able to demonstrate a knowledge and affection for the animals they hunted
    • The tendency of artists to represent animals in twisted perspective or composite view that combines frontal and profile views of animals because want an image that is completely informative (help in the hunt?)
    • Often lack common ground line
  • Early Sculpture
    • The first sculptures were found in Europe, but sculptural works being produced in early cultures around the globe
    • Some are even older than Namibian-painted plaques
    • No one is really sure what their functions were or what the artisans had in mind when creating them
    • Researches can only speculate because before had writing
    • More figures of women than men
    • Figures of women shown as nude although historians believe people of this time did wear clothing
    • Sculpture could be functional objects as well
  • The Neolithic Period (9,000 BC to 3,000 BC)
    • Around 9000 B.C. the Ice Age gave way to a transitional period, the Neolithic- Europe became climactically, geographically, and biologically much like it is today.
    • Humans began to settle in fixed abodes and domesticate plants and animals. A culture developed and produced art that originated from cave paintings
    • Paintings of humans and animals in scenes of hunt, battle, ritual dance, and harvest have been found on stone walls of shallow rock shelters along the east coast of Spain and Africa
    • Global sculptural tradition continues
    • Once food supply became more secure, they changed from hunters to herders, farmers, and villagers.
    • The transition to the Neolithic period began in the ancient Near East
    • Artists built the earth into monuments of stone and clay.
    • Walls of dwellings were decorated as well as functional objects (i.e. clay pots)
    • Small stone or terracotta statuettes created as well as subtly modeled human skulls reconstructed in plaster with inlaid seashells for eyes and painted hair
    • Remains of oldest known settled communities in the grassy uplands bordering the Tigris and Euphrates Rivers in Mesopotamia (portions of what is now Iraq and Syria)
    • Regions of early settlements provided necessary preconditions for development of agriculture- plentiful species of native plants (wheat, barley, rice, corn), rain, fertile soil
    • In addition to agriculture, new sedentary societies originated weaving, metalworking, pottery, and counting and recording tokens
  • Europe in the Neolithic Period
    • While Paleolithic paintings and sculptures abound, no comparably developed towns of time of Catal Hoyuk
    • Local Neolithic populations in several areas developed a monumental architecture employing massive rough-cut stones
    • Megaliths- ‚Äúgreat stones‚Äùü°™ megalithic culture
    • Plentiful megalithic monuments in Europe
    • Arrangement of huge stones in a circle (cromlech or henge), often surrounded by a ditch, almost entirely limited to Britain

  • Concepts and Main Ideas
    • Life for the Greek was a glorious struggle-their art focuses on the events and achievements in this life
    • "Future ages will wonder at us, as the present age wonders at us now." Pericles
    • "For we are lovers of the beautiful, yet simple in our tastes, and we cultivate the mind without the loss of manliness‚Ä¶[we are] the school of Hellas" Pericles
    • Greek cultural focus- ideal humanistic education and life
    • The humanistic worldview led the Greeks to create the concept of democracy
    • Greeks believed in many gods and goddesses who differed only from human beings in that they were immortal
    • Creating the perfect individual became the Greek ideal
    • Finest products of Greek civilization created in Athens
    • Athenian aim was to achieve a balance of intellectual and physical discipline, an ideal of humanistic education-"a sound mind in a sound body"
  • Geometric/ Orientalizing Period (7th century BCE)
    • Disintegration of Bronze Age social order and destruction of Mycenaean palaces send Greece into a Dark Age- loss of knowledge of how to read, write, cut stone/ masonry, build citadels and tombs, paint frescos, and sculpt in stone- also period of depopulation- conditions too unsettled to allow art to develop- production of some ceramics continues during this Dark Age
    • In 8th century, economic conditions improve and population begins to grow- Greeks break out of isolation and begin to trade with other cultures
    • Arts reemerge beginning with ceramics and small bronze figures
    • Artistic period gets the name "Geometric" from heavy reliance on use of simple geometric shapes and forms for decoration of vessels and creation of forms
    • Sometimes called "Orientalizing" period because of Greek trade with the Ancient Near East
  • Archaic Period (600-480 BCE)
    • See enormous influence of Egyptian canon on the art and architecture of the Greeks
    • Continuous refinement of human form in sculpture, but no indication of weight shift in stances
    • Temples begin to be constructed of more permanent materials like marble and limestone- use models of Egyptian columnar halls like hypostyle hall from Temple of Amen-Re at Karnak
    • Figural sculpture plays major role in decoration of temples- partly to tell about deity who resided within and partly to serve the idea of temple as a votive offering to deity
    • Athenian Agora first constructed- functioned as a center of all community life
    • Use of the Archaic smile
  • The Early (480-450 BCE) and High (450-400 BCE) Classical Periods
    • Classical Period begins with the Greek defeat of the Persians
    • Decades following the removal of the Persian threat are generally considered the high point of Greek Civilization
    • Early Classical period is sometimes called the "Severe Style" because in both attitude and dress their figures are more serious and have a severity that contrasts with the smiling and elaborately dressed figures of the Late Archaic period
    • Early Classical sculptors are the 1st to break away from the rigid and unnatural Egyptian-inspired pose of the Archaic Kourous figures
    • Focus on community and the ideal world of perfect beings and perfect buildings
    • A change in posture in sculpture can be seen in figures from the Temple of Zeus and the Athenian Acropolis as sculptors become more concerned with how people really stand
    • Use of contrapposto ("weight shift") becomes the norm (ex.Kritios Boy) as natural motion in space replaces the rigid frontality of the Archaic Period
    • Polykleitos's treatise lays out the rules for creating a perfect statue by using mathematical formulae and calculations. His work Doryphorous (Spear Bearer) was created to illustrate his treatise.
    • Emphasis on rationality, mathematical ratios, symmetry, and balance in sculpture and architecture
    • Period of Pericles and construction of Athenian Acropolis complex
    • Use of heavy "wet" drapery to define the figure underneath the clothing
    • Only painting we have from this period is vase painting, which also shows a focus on naturalism and accuracy through the use of foreshortening- no more composite views with frontal eyes on profile heads
    • In vase painting see the addition of landscape elements and elimination of single ground line which is tremendous break from previous periods which relied heavily on a common ground line which all the figures stood/ sat/ moved on
    • The art from this period reflects a peaceful, serene idealism- there is no show of emotion
  • Late Classical Period (400-323 BCE)
    • Peloponnesian War (431-404 BCE) left Greece weak
    • This war and the political strife that would follow brought an end to the serene idealism of 5th century Greek Art
    • Art begins to focus more on the individual and real world appearance
    • Themes of High Classical period still used- still depicted gods and goddesses with superhuman beauty, but lose some of their solemn grandeur and take on sense of worldly sensuousness that illustrates humanizing spirit of the culture- they are no longer awesome and remote figures
    • Focus on a beauty that appeals more to the eye than to the mind- no longer focus on majestic strength and rational design
    • Throughout Archaic and Early and High Classical periods, Greek sculptors shared common goals, but in Late Classical period of the 4th century, have distinctive individual styles develop
    • 3 major Late Classical Sculptors- Praxiteles, Skopas of Paros, and Lysippos of Sikyon
    • Period of Alexander the Great and the Macedonian Court- end date for Late Classical period is the year Alexander died (323 BCE)
    • Use of mosaics for decoration popular
  • Hellenistic Period (323-31 BCE)
    • Alexander conquered Egypt and Ancient Near East, creating a vast empire which ended up being divided among his generals after his death
    • There were several cultural centers/ court cities in the Greek Empire- Antioch (Syria), Pergamon (Asia Minor), Alexandria (Egypt)
    • An international culture and the Greek language united the Hellenistic world
    • Time of small, heroic city-states passed away along with power and prestige of Athens- a cosmopolitan society took their place
    • After death of Alexander, empire divided up among Hellenistic kings who became rich on spoils of war and goods from the East
    • Greater variety, complexity, and sophistication of the Hellenistic culture called for architecture on an imperial scale with a great degree of diversity- there were larger needs to serve and requirements to meet than what had previously been expected of the Classical city-states
    • Period ends with defeat of Anthony and Cleopatra and Greek fall to Roman power
    • Building activity shifts from Greek mainland to opulent cities of the Hellenistic monarchs in Asia Minor because locations more central to the entire Hellenistic world
    • Architecture characterized by great scale, theatrical element of surprise, & willingness to break away from old canons of temple design
    • In sculpture, much more drama and action- tumultuous narratives, violent movement, swirling draperies, vivid depictions of death and suffering- dramatic motion and emotion
    • Use of very deep carving on sculptures to create dark shadow- this creates heightened sense of drama using play of light and dark
    • Twisting, theatrical gestures and emotional intensity of works reflect political turbulence as empire begins to decline and attempts to fend off Romans
    • Rejection of idea that a statue has to be ideally a proportioned, self-contained entity on a bare pedestal- Hellenistic works interact with their environments and appear as living, breathing, and intensely emotive human/ divine presences

    • Late Antiquity unit 3

  • The Art of Late Antiquity - Background
    • A diverse population of Roman Empire - range of social, ethnic, racial, linguistic, and religious backgrounds
    • During 3rd and 4th century, shift from polytheism to monotheism
    • Dura-Europos - founded shortly after death of Alexander the Great; ‚ÄúPompei of the desert;‚Äù microcosm of different religious currents of late antiquity; extensive mural program in Synagogue; Christian community house
  • Early Christianity
    • Christianity attractive for the poor b/c found promise in the afterlife; also rich and poor have equal status and judged equally in the afterlife
    • Christians persecuted by the Romans because:
      • Christians had alien beliefs about the incarnation of a god as a man and his resurrection to save the human race
      • Christians refused to pay even token homage to Roman state‚Äôs official gods
      • Romans feared Christianity would weaken imperial authority
    • Romans cremated their dead; Christians opposed to cremation because of their views on the afterlife - increase in number of sarcophagi as Christianity grew; proper burial important
    • Early examples of early Christian art is from the catacombs
    • Edict of Milan in 313 - end persecution of Christians - can worship openly and convert people - prior to this, Christianity had status of a sect or cult
    • Christians buried outside the city - families would join money together and buy land for burial - create Christian confraternity
    • Some early Christian churches built on sites above catacombs so could worship at burial site of early Christian martyrs
  • Early Christian Motifs and Iconography
    • Art of early Christianity is primarily for funerary purposes - decorate catacombs and sarcophagi
    • Themes of interest include the Good Shepherd, scenes form the Life of Christ, Old Testament miracles which prefigure the sacrifice and resurrection of Christ (exs - story of Jonah and the whale, sacrifice of Isaac, story of Adam and Eve), orant figures
    • Ceilings of chapel areas in catacombs (cubicula) painted in the ‚Äúspoke-and-wheel‚Äù style seen before in Ostia - use a central medallion and lunettes to create design
    • Images of God and Christ not used - concern over creating ‚Äúgraven images,‚Äù which is against the teachings in the Bible
  • Early Christian Architecture
    • Once Christianity received imperial approval under Constantine, there is a need for Christian churches
    • Christian churches had to meet requirements of Christian liturgy, provide a suitable place to celebrate the Christian faith, and accommodate the growing number of worshippers
    • Roman emperor and obligated to protect ancient Roman religion, traditions, etc, however, Constantine was eager to provide buildings and burial sites for Christian rituals - he is 1st major patron of Christian architecture and builds basilicas, memorials, mausoleum for Christians in Rome, Constantinople and other sacred sites of the religion like Bethlehem and Jerusalem
    • Old St. Peter‚Äôs - greatest of all of Constantine‚Äôs churches; on spot where believed St. Peter was buried - built over old cemetery; could hold 3000-4000 worshippers; resembles Roman basilicas and audience halls like Basilica Ulpia in Trajan‚Äôs forum - do not follow Greco-Roman temple design; **know plan of Old St. Peter‚Äôs
    • Basilica plans (St. Peter‚Äôs) vs. Central plans (Santa Costanza)
  • The Art of Mosaics
    • First mosaics used pebbles set into cement - designs were uncomplicated, confined to geometric shapes, and generally monochrome - lead strips were used for linear definition
    • In mid 3rd century, new kind of mosaic invented that used tesserae (tiny cut stones) - this gave the artist a lot more flexibility b/c could adjust size and shape of pieces to fit the needs of their designs - no longer have to use lead strips in design to show contours and interior details
    • By 4th century, mosaics had developed a high level of sophistication - used broad range of colors, shading, setting, and clothing to suggest 3-d volume
    • Tesserae allow for greater gradations in color like we saw in Battle of Issus mosaic from Pompei
  • Early Christian Mosaics
    • Used for decorating interiors of churches
    • Feature both Old and New Testament stories and iconography
    • Tesserae used in Early Christian mosaics made of cut glass - makes surfaces sparkle; Romans preferred opaque marble pieces instead
    • Standard way to decorate walls and vaults in Early Christian buildings b/c caught the light flooding in through the windows and gave vibrant reflection - this produced sharp contrasts and concentrations of color that could focus attention on the most central, relevant features of a composition
    • Not meant to incorporate subtle tonal changes of naturalistic painter‚Äôs approach - colors are placed, not blended - this created bright, hard, glittering texture with a rigidly simplified pattern
    • Mostly situated in high places, so use larger tesserae than floor mosaics and pieces set unevenly so surfaces could catch and reflect light
    • Used simple design so easy to read from the ground

Unit 4 Byzantine Art

  • Byzantine Background
    • Constantine founded a ‚Äúnew Rome‚Äù in Constantinople on the old Greek city of Byzantium in 324 - changed name to Constantinople
    • Roman Empire falls apart in 5th century - 2 emperors established - one ruled the West from Milan and Ravenna (capital would be moved); the other ruled the East from Constantinople - this division is permanent
    • Eastern Christian Empire called ‚ÄúByzantium‚Äù - use the term ‚ÄúByzantine‚Äù to identify whatever pertains to this empire as far as territory, history, and culture but Byzantine emperors did not use this term to refer to themselves - still called themselves ‚ÄúRomans‚Äù
    • Spoke Greek, not Latin like in the West
    • Emperors never relinquish their claims as legitimate successors of Roman emperors
    • Religion of empire was Orthodox Christianity - official faith
    • Emperors considered quasi-divine
    • Hard to make absolute political and religious unity a reality because still had a diverse population
  • Early Byzantine Art (527-726)
    • Golden Age under reign of Justinian and his consort, Empress Theodora
    • Art is a cultural mix of Roman, Christian, and eastern influences in the art of Byzantium.
    • Rise of Justinian marks the end of the late Roman Empire and the beginning of the Byzantine Empire
    • Byzantine art emerges as a novel and distinctive style - leaves behind the feelings of uncertainty and hesitation of Eastern Christian Art
    • Can recognize sources in Late Antique Art but had new and independent power of invention - expressed unique character of the Eastern Christian cult of Constantine
    • Justinian held his own as home and abroad against all challenges to his power - starts ambitious building program - costs a lot of money for the people in taxes - these buildings defined Byzantine style in architecture forever
    • Art is ideal vehicle for conveying extremely complex symbolism of fully developed Christian dogma
    • Shapes and figures have lost volume of earlier mosaics - have flat silhouettes with linear details - effect is rich, flat tapestry without illusionistic devices
    • Use of gold for backgrounds - very few blue skies and little use of architectural or landscape elements - gold creates otherworldly feel and give perspective of paradise of afterlife
    • Little use of cast shadows or modeling in light and shadow - any attempt at shading looks like stripes
  • Byzantine Painting
    • Known for its jewel-like treatment of the manuscripts
    • Manuscript Painters were highly trained to execute minute details with precision
    • Most employ the same use of gold seen in icons*
    • Manuscripts= Status, very few could read. Libraries are true temples of learning
  • Icon worship & the iconoclasm (726-843)
    • Opposition to icon worship particularly strong in some areas
    • At the time, faithful would burn incense, kneel in front of icons, and pray for protection, cure for illness, etc - purpose of icon to evoke presence of holy figures people addressing in their prayers
    • Icon veneration becomes confused with idol worship
    • Series of crises in the 7th and 8th centuries left Byzantium fighting for survival - this ends both period of Imperial Rome and the Early Byzantine period
    • In 7th centuries, series of calamities erupted which indirectly caused a ban on images - Byzantine Empire lost 2/3 of its territory - lost cities, wealth, people - Pope Leo III of Christian Roman Empire said God was punishing them for idolatrous worship, and in 726, he prohibited the use of images - very little religious figurative art for the next 100 years
  • Art and the Iconoclasm (726-843)
    • Instead of images, use symbols like cross, vacant throne of heaven, cabinet with scriptural rolls, etc - stylized floral, animal, and architectural motifs used as decorative fill
    • Not only nonew images, but old images destroyed in Iconoclasm
    • Iconoclasts (breakers of images) and iconophiles (lovers of images)
    • Have little info about art in this period because so much destroyed
  • Middle Byzantine Art (843-1204)
    • In 9th century, powerful reaction against Iconoclasm
    • Destruction of images condemned as heresy
    • Restoration of images began in 843
    • Art, literature, and learning become important again
    • Period of great renovation and revival in every aspect of Byzantine life
    • Byzantine Empire becomes a major cultural and artistic center
    • Emphasis on classical models
    • Byzantine art becomes more symbolic and less naturalistic
    • More emphasis on line, pattern, and color
    • Images no longer flat and two-dimensional, but more sculptural and three-dimensional
    • Modeling in light and shadow becomes more important
    • Byzantine art becomes more abstract and less literal
    • Icons more expressive and vibrant
    • Byzantine artists create a new style that is more abstract, otherworldly, and timeless
  • Byzantine Architecture
    • Byzantine architecture is grand and imposing
    • Emphasis on decoration and ornamentation
    • Use of domes, vaults, and arches
    • Structures built to impress and inspire the viewer
    • Constantinople becomes the center of Byzantine architecture
    • Hagia Sophia is the most famous Byzantine building
    • Built in 537 by Emperor Justinian and his court architects Anthemius of Tralles and Isidorus of Miletus
    • Hagia Sophia is a cathedral and later a mosque
    • Combines the Greek cross plan with a huge dome supported by four piers
    • Dome is supported by pendentives, a revolutionary architectural feature that allows the dome to sit on top of the square space of the church
    • Interior is adorned with mosaics, frescoes, and marble
    • Exterior is plain and unadorned
    • Other notable Byzantine buildings include the Church of the Holy Apostles, the Monastery of St. John Studius, and the Church of the Pantokrator
  • Byzantine Textile and Metalwork
    • Byzantine textiles are luxurious and ornate
    • Use of gold thread and intricate embroidery
    • Textiles used in clothing, hangings, and altar cloths
    • Byzantine metalwork is equally impressive
    • Use of gold, silver, and bronze in jewelry, religious objects, and secular objects
    • Notable examples include the Codex Aureus of St. Emmeram, the Madonna and Child Enthroned, and the Mshatta Facade
  • Byzantine Illuminated Manuscripts
    • Illuminated manuscripts are handwritten books with decorative borders and illustrations
    • Byzantine illuminated manuscripts are known for their ornate gold backgrounds and elaborate illustrations
    • Used in the production of religious texts, such as the Bible and the Psalter
    • Notable examples include the Gospel Book of Kinburn, the Madrid Skylitzes, and the Vienna Dioscurides
  • Byzantine Iconography
    • Icons are religious images, usually of Jesus, Mary, and the saints
    • Used in Orthodox churches for veneration and prayer
    • Byzantine icons follow strict rules for representation and are highly symbolic
    • Subjects are usually depicted in frontal poses with halos and long robes
    • Colors and clothing have specific meanings
    • Notable examples include the Christ Pantocrator, the Theotokos of Vladimir, and the Hodegetria
  • Byzantine Music and Literature
    • Byzantine music is an integral part of Orthodox liturgy and is characterized by its use of the Greek language and its emphasis on the repetition of melodic formulas
    • Byzantine literature includes works in Greek, such as the poetry of John Geometres and the historiography of Michael Psellos, as well as translations of Latin works into Greek, such as the Corpus Dionysiacum
    • Byzantine literature also includes hagiography, or biographies of saints, and theological treatises
  • Byzantine Science and Technology
    • Byzantine scientists made important contributions to fields such as mathematics, astronomy, and medicine
    • Byzantine technology includes innovations such as the hydropowered sawmill, the Greek fire weapon, and the use of Greek fire in naval warfare
  • Byzantine Society and Culture
    • Byzantine society was hierarchical, with the emperor at the top and peasants at the bottom
    • Byzantine culture was influenced by Roman, Greek, and Eastern traditions
    • Byzantine court culture was lavish and ornate, with a strong emphasis on ceremony and protocol
    • Byzantine cuisine was influenced by Greek, Roman, and Middle Eastern culinary traditions and featured dishes such as lamb, poultry, fish, vegetables, and sweets
  • Middle Byzantine Art (843-1204)
    • In 9th century, powerful reaction against Iconoclasm
    • Destruction of images condemned as heresy
    • Restoration of images began in 843
    • Art, literature, and learning become important again
    • Period of great renovation
    • Basil I- head of new dynasty- thought of himself as restorer of Roman Empire
    • During Iconoclasm, many Byzantine artists went West and and found employment in the courts of the Germanic kings- these artists strongly influence character of western European art
    • Under Basil and his successors, plenty of commissions for artists-mural painters, mosaicists, ivory carvers, metal workers
    • Begin restoring churches defaced and neglected during period of Iconoclasm
  • Late Byzantine Period (1204-1453)
    • In 11th and 12th centuries, change in power result in Turks taking over part of the Empire- Byzantine Orthodox Church finally splits completely from Church of Rome
    • Crusades bring people from the Latin West into Byzantine lands to fight Muslims- passed through Constantinople several times- made note of the wealth and magnificence of its cities
    • During 4th crusade (1203-1204), Latin/ western crusaders attack Constantinople out of envy, greed, religious fanaticism, and ethnic enmity- they take over area- set up own kingdoms within Empire and split Byzantium into 3 small states
    • Despite turmoil, arts flourish well into 14th century
  • Late Byzantine Art (1204-1453)
    • Luxury Arts still play important role- metalworking, ivory carving, illuminated manuscripts- create opulent d√©cor for celebrating the faith- there is great richness of texture and color in Byzantine churches
    • Painting shows a new burst of creative energy- masterpieces in both mural and icon painting
    • Figures float in spiritual atmosphere of gold background- they have no material mass and no volume to cast a shadow
    • Smoothness and lightness of modeling and coloration of figures
    • No longer have jagged abstractions of drapery seen in Early Byzantine frescoes and mosaics- return to fluid delineation of drapery based on tradition of classical illusionism
    • Byzantine artists looked to Greco-Roman tradition of illusionism but they are not concerned with systematic observation and recording of material nature because trying to represent the eternal
    • Artists drew images of a persistent and conventionalized vision of spiritual world which is unsusceptible to change
    • This vision is the consistent factor that unites Byzantine art from 6th century to 14th century

    Early Medieval Art unit 4

  • Early Medieval Period
    • The period between 400-1400 was referred to as Dark Ages because it was considered rough, uncivilized, crude, and primitive artistically
    • The period is thought of as the blank between two civilizations- the Roman Empire and the Renaissance
    • Most scholars stopped seeing the art of this period as unsophisticated long ago
    • The early Middle Ages is the period from 500-1000- art from this period represents a fusion of Christianity, Greco-Roman heritage, and the cultures of the non-Roman peoples North of the Alps
    • Over centuries, a new order replaces what had been the Roman empire, involving the merging of Roman and non-Roman cultures which developed their own social and political institutions
  • Artistic Overview of the Early Medieval Period
    • As the Roman empire declined, there were conflicts with non-European peoples competing for political authority such as the Huns, Vandals, Merovingians, Franks, and Goths (also called the Warrior Lords Period)
    • We have no idea of the full range of art of these peoples, but do have some surviving works- primarily small "status symbols" like weapons and items of personal adornment- most of which are characteristics of prestige ornaments like the fibula
    • These items show a great degree of technical and stylistic sophistication
    • The Christianization of the British Isles leads to a hybrid artistic style combining Viking aesthetics with Christian iconography
    • Monasteries were centers of artistic production, including illuminated manuscripts
    • There was an attempt in the later part of the period to unify territory and recapture the greatness of the Christian Roman Empire- the Holy Roman Empire began under Charlemagne and continued to the 19th century as a major power in Europe
    • Art from this period has an overall sense of an attempt to revive the classical past by using classical models, but does not have the naturalism or realism of antiquity- the focus is on Christian dogma and imperial power
  • The Vikings (Warrior Lords) and their Art
    • In 793, the Vikings set sail for Scandinavia and landed in the British Isles
    • They destroyed Christian monastic communities at Lindisfarne Island and also attacked monasteries in Scotland and England
    • From 793 until the 11th century, the Vikings were a terror in Western Europe
    • They had exceptional talent for organization and administration as well as war
    • The art of the Viking sea rovers is associated with their great wooden ships and examples of their art can be found in ship burials
    • The artistic traditions of the Vikings in Europe were applied to a variety of media such as wood, stone, and metal- much of their art consists of highly decorated functional objects
    • Imagery in the artwork includes intertwined ribbon-like designs called interlacing as well as abstract animal imagery- these design features reflect the cultures of pre-Christian Northern Europe
  • Hiberno-Saxon Art
    • In 432, St. Patrick established the church in Ireland and christianized the Celts
    • Because of geographic isolation, the monastic orders in Ireland differed from the Church of Rome in liturgical practices and holidays
    • Irish monks set up monasteries in Britain and Scotland and began to convert people
    • Hiberno-Saxon art is also referred to as insular art because it comes from the Irish-English islands
    • Insular art flourished in monasteries
    • The most distinctive product of insular art was illuminated manuscripts- books were the primary way of christianizing the British Isles
    • Illuminations brought the word of God to primarily illiterate people
    • Books were handwritten and hand-copied by monks, so they are very scarce and regarded as treasures
    • Different types of books produced during this time include the Pentateuch, Gospels, lectionaries, benedictorials, and the Book of Hours
  • Hiberno-Saxon Illuminated Manuscripts
    • Hiberno-Saxon illuminated manuscripts are known for their use of interlace patterns (horror vacui) and animal style, a medieval art form in which animals are depicted in a stylized and complicated pattern, fighting with one another
    • Each section of the manuscripts starts with an oversized initial
    • The manuscripts also feature rich color fields
    • The artists of these books had an exceptional handling of color and form

Unit 5 Romanesque Art

  • Background on the Romanesque Era
    • The Romanesque era is the first period since the Archaic and Classical Greek periods to take its name from an artistic style rather than politics or geography
    • "Romanesque" is a title invented by art historians to describe an artistic phenomenon/style of this particular time period- it means "Roman-like"
    • The term was first used in the 19th century to describe European architecture of the 11th and 12th centuries because certain architectural elements like barrel vaults and groin vaults resembled ancient Roman architecture
    • The term broadly designates the 150 year period from 1050-1200
    • During the early Middle Ages, there was a feudal system in place, but the growth of towns and cities during the Romanesque period gradually replaced the feudal system as the governing political, social, and economic system of the Late Middle Ages
    • Monasteries and churches were separated from the busy secular life of Romanesque towns
    • During the 11th and 12th centuries, thousands of ecclesiastical buildings were either remodeled or newly built and the construction of churches became an obsession
    • The economic investment in religious buildings and their furnishings was also a reflection of the significant increase in pilgrim traffic in Romanesque Europe- pilgrims were an important source of revenue for monasteries that possessed relics of venerated saints
    • Romanesque architecture incorporates the widespread use of stone vaults in 11th and 12th century churches, which inspired the term "Romanesque"
    • Despite regional variations, all Romanesque buildings manifest a new, widely shared method of architectural thinking- a new logic of design and construction
  • Background: The Impact of Pilgrimages (continued)
    • Pilgrimage was most conspicuous feature of public devotion- proclamation of a pilgrim‚Äôs faith
    • Major shrines were St. Peter‚Äôs and St. Paul‚Äôs in Rome and Church of the Holy Sepulcher in Jerusalem
    • Visitors braved bad roads and hostile forests/wildernesses which were filled with robbers- did it for salvation's sake
    • Many times, people undertook pilgrimage as an act of repentance or because they were seeking healing
    • Pilgrims fostered the growth of towns
    • Pilgrims were the primary and conceptual catalyst for the art and architecture of the Romanesque period
  • Important Elements of Romanesque Architecture
    • Stone vaulting was used to replace timber roofs to help with fire-proofing and acoustics
    • The nave, transept, and side aisles were added to existing churches or expanded to accommodate larger crowds of pilgrims
    • The crossing square, where the nave meets the transept, may be covered by a dome and also functions as the basic unit of measure for building
    • Transverse arches are rounded arches that break up the nave into bays
    • Bays are units that divide the nave and create a modular plan for the church- they may be barrel-vaulted (earlier) or groin-vaulted (later)
    • The ambulatory is a major innovation that allows pilgrims to walk behind the mass in the apse and visit radiating chapels- it facilitates the viewing of relics
    • Radiating chapels are off of the ambulatory and are used to hold relics
    • The gallery/tribune level was added to accommodate overflow crowds
    • The clerestory was used to allow light in and is usually small in Romanesque churches
    • A barrel vault is the norm for vaulting in Romanesque naves- bays are vaulted (not a continuous barrel vault for the length of the nave)
    • A groin vault is less common for vaulting in Romanesque churches and is used more in side aisles- it will eventually replace barrel-vaulting in the nave in the Gothic period
    • The portal and its parts are the entranceway into the church and are decorated with sculptures that were used to educate the largely illiterate public about salvation and damnation
    • The cloister is an enclosed garden or meditative space with an ambulatory found in monastic abbey churches
    • A cruciform structure is cross-shaped and is commonly seen in medieval churches with the addition of a transept- it is also symbolic for Christians
  • Romanesque Churches
    • Romanesque churches have specific characteristics, including: a basilica plan with a transept, increased nave length, extended or double side aisles, an apse with an ambulatory, radiating chapels, upper level galleries (tribunes), barrel vaulting (later groin vaulting), compound piers, and a segmented vaulted interior space
    • Romanesque architects made many changes to early Christian church plans in order to accommodate large numbers of congregants and to help ensure the building's longevity, including increasing the nave length and extending the aisles
  • Painting
    • The art of painting did not need to be "revived" because it was still alive in illuminated manuscripts and mural painting
    • There are an unprecedented number of preserved frescoes and illuminated books from this time period
    • Just like architecture and sculpture, there was regional and stylistic diversity in painting
    • Despite Muslim control across the Iberian Peninsula (present-day Spain and Portugal), there was still Christianity and Christian art
    • Romanesque painting is still lacking in realism in the modeling of figures and drapery, with elongated human forms and schematized patterns for fabric folds
    • There was a great demand for illuminated manuscripts and an instinct for decorative surface patterns

    • Unit 5 Gothic Art

  • Background on Gothic Period
    • The term "Gothic" was first used in the mid-16th century by Giorgio Vasari, the "father of art history," to describe late medieval art and architecture- he thought it was "monstrous and barbarous" art like something invented by the Goths
    • This idea reinforces the fact that people thought of the Middle Ages as a period of artistic decline
    • In the 13th and 14th centuries, this style was "all the rage" in most of Europe- it was still thought of as "modern" or "French"
    • The clergy and lay public alike regarded new cathedrals as images of the city of God, the Heavenly Jerusalem- they felt they had the privilege to create this on Earth
    • The style first appears in Northern France in 1140, and there is some overlap with the Romanesque period
    • By the 13th century, the style from the region around Paris had spread throughout Europe
    • Gothic would become internationally acclaimed, but was largely a regional style
    • It is important to remember that in the eastern and southern parts of Europe, Islamic and Byzantine styles were flourishing
    • The Gothic period was a time of both prosperity and turmoil in Europe- there was the plague/Black Death, the Great Schism (two popes), and a time of profound change- the focus of intellectual and religious life shifted from monasteries in the country and pilgrimage churches to the rapidly expanding cities with great new cathedrals reaching up to the sky
  • Gothic: How it Started
    • There were several factors that contributed to the emergence of the Gothic style, including peace and prosperity from 987-1328, the growing wealth of cities and towns, a shift in focus from agriculture to goods and services, and the emergence of schools in Paris (which became an intellectual capital)
    • Three crucial historical events also played a role in the development of the Gothic style: the Hundred Years' War (France vs England, which left France ruined), the Babylonian Captivity (in which the Pope left Rome and the city fell, leading to a period of conflict known as the Great Schism), and the Black Death of 1348 (a plague in which an estimated 1/4 to 1/3 of the world's population died- this event was seen as punishment from God and led to a return to religion)
